How Diffusion Models Learn

Add noise, then teach a model
to undo it.

Diffusion is how Midjourney, Stable Diffusion, Veo 3, and Runway work under the hood. The whole training trick fits in one diagram — and once you see it, the rest of image and video AI gets a lot less mysterious.

Figure from Jay Alammar's The Illustrated Stable Diffusion — the canonical visual explainer

Step 1 · The trick

One image becomes many training examples.

Take a clean image. Add a little noise → that's training example one. Add more noise → example two. Keep going until the image is pure static. Now one image has given you five (or fifty, or a thousand) different training samples at different noise levels.

Do this for every image in your dataset and you suddenly have an enormous supervised training set — for free, no human labeling required.

Step 2 · What the model learns

Given a noisy image, predict the noise.

The model's job is simple: look at one of these noisy images, look at how much noise was added (the "noise level"), and predict what the noise was.

At inference time, you flip it around: start with pure noise, ask the model "what's the noise here?", subtract its prediction, repeat for many steps. What you're left with is a clean image — one the model has essentially hallucinated into existence from random static.

— A few honest caveats if you're presenting this
  1. Real diffusion models don't use 5 noise levels — they use ~1,000. Alammar shows 5 for the diagram. The real noise schedule (linear or cosine over t = 0…1000) is what makes the math work out for smooth denoising.
  2. Stable Diffusion specifically does this in latent space, not pixel space. An encoder first compresses the image to a smaller representation, then diffusion operates on the latents. Same idea, much cheaper compute.
  3. The text prompt isn't in this diagram, but it's threaded in via cross-attention — a separate text encoder (often CLIP) tells the noise-predictor "you should be denoising toward an image of X."
Why this matters for the deck

LLMs predict the next word. Diffusion models predict the next denoising step. Different training signal, completely different math — but the same underlying idea: learn from data, not from rules.
